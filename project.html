In a whimsical blend of astrology and economics, I embarked on a journey to explore whether our daily horoscopes might hint at the fate of our financial investments. For this playful endeavor, I began with two datasets in hand: daily horoscope readings and stock market data for the year 2016. Using sentiment analysis tools like ROBERTA and VADERS, each horoscope was classified into one of three categories: positive, neutral, or negative. The next logical step? Compare this sentiment with the daily stock market performance. On days when a positive sentiment coincided with a market rise (and vice versa for negative sentiments), the horoscope was dubbed "correct" for the day. In the initial stages, a granular, day-to-day analysis revealed an accuracy rate of 50%, akin to the expected outcome of a coin flip. In simpler terms, on a day-to-day basis, it seemed horoscope sentiments might not be more predictive than mere chance. However, curiosity led me further. What if, instead of individual days, I considered broader intervals? Averaging out sentiments and market movements over 2-day, 3-day, and even up to 30-day intervals produced a surprising observation: the accuracy rate leapt from the initial 50% to an intriguing 80%! So, what could be the cause behind this surge in accuracy with grouped intervals? A few hypotheses come to mind: By grouping days, short-term market fluctuations, which are often noise, get averaged out, revealing more general, overarching trends. Historically, stock markets have shown a tendency to rise over longer periods. Hence, these broader groupings naturally capture this overall bullish tendency, making it align more closely with generally positive sentiments. Larger groupings ensure that extreme outliers have a diminished impact, leading to more stable, predictable patterns. While this uptick in accuracy is undoubtedly fascinating, it's vital to approach these findings with a pinch of salt. The alignment in patterns doesn't necessarily indicate causation. It's entirely plausible that these correlations are coincidental. Additionally, tailoring methodologies purely based on observed results can occasionally lead to overfitting, meaning the approach might work for this specific dataset but not generalize well to new, unseen data. In the vast, intricate dance of the stock market, countless factors play a role, from geopolitics and corporate decisions to natural disasters. A single day's horoscope might not encompass this multifaceted complexity. Thus, while our findings offer an entertaining perspective, the union of stars and stocks remains a delightful enigma.
